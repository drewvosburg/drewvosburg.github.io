MIRS

Senior Product Designer, Consultant

For United States Military Entrance, I helped to develop interfaces and standards for a next generation digital system to help the military assess applicants.

The Problem

Military Entrance Processing Stations were using printouts with full social security numbers, and software built in the 90s which was meant only for data entry and not clarity or scannability.

My goal was to help them confidently understand the status of an applicant in front of them, and to protect applicants' personal information.

Constraints

This was a large-scale government project, affecting more than 60 locations and over a hundred thousand applicants per year. There are strict personal and health data privacy requirements for applicant data, which comes from paper forms. We weren’t permitted to change forms or policy.

Built to scan

Users have an easy to scan visual status for each of the key dimensions of applicant qualification.

Clear visual path to success

There is a clear visual narrative of the big steps for an applicant, from start to finish.

Data entry workflows

Data permissions are carefully managed to protect privacy and reduce risk of abuse. Only employees with legitimate business reasons can access sensitive medical information or social security numbers.

Outcomes

  • Average 45% faster time-to-task for applicant scheduling activities
  • 30% fewer scheduling errors than the previous system

In testing, government user said she would rather do all data input manually with the new system than do just half of the work manually and the other half automated, as it was in the previous system.

"If I had to process twice as many applications in the new system compared to the old system, I still would prefer it. I can see the status and it’s clear what I need to do."

- Employee

"I would rather do twice as much of my work manually in the new system than doing my current workload in the old system."

- Employee

What would I do differently

  • I would spend more careful time and energy on content design
  • New user patterns and lightweight onboarding screens would help simplify training
