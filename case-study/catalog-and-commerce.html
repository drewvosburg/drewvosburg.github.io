Facebook Commerce and Ad Catalog merger

Senior Product Designer

Two business-facing tools were merged into one, simplifying and strengthening the Facebook commerce experience.

The Problem

Facebook’s business tools for commerce and advertising were fragmented.

Several key user tasks were duplicated across multiple tools, with similar concepts that weren’t compatible with each other.

Some tasks forced users to switch between platforms in the middle of the workflow, taking up to ten seconds to load, and leaving users stranded in the wrong tool when they were done.

Constraints

The stakes for making a change were high. It was critical not to hurt ads revenue or sales with any new experience, and the rollout affected critical revenue for over half a million businesses.

User personas were complex and elusive. Some edge-case businesses have hundreds of catalogs, multiple commerce accounts, Instagram handles and Facebook pages. Other businesses only run ads and never use additional commerce tools. Navigation needed to remain useful for both users.

Pare down

I broke down navigation for the two tools, compared and contrasted, then merged them together.

This reduced the mental model for users, and as a bonus cut down the technical scope, helping the changes go live faster.

Speed up

Because of the scope of the new navigation, there were only a few pages that needed to transition technical frameworks.

As a result of the newly shared framework, page load times were dramatically reduced, making switching between tabs take far less time, smoothing out the experience.

Outcomes

As a result of merging the tools for managing products and commerce, use of the tools increased across the board. There was an uplift in businesses setting up ads for the first time, as well as an uplift in businesses using insights, which is correlated with better ads performance and sales.

"Overall it looks very strong. It is really like combining these two into a stronger platform."

- Lululemon

What would I do differently

While we reduced the number of broader tools from five to three, there are still three tools. The result of inertia is that we have the same fundamental problem, slightly less aggravated, but with significantly more business users. Instead of building a plane in the air, it would have been a good investment to push harder for even more aggressive platform consolidation.
